# DAVID KNOTT BLOG VOICE & PRONOUN PATTERNS
## Comprehensive Style Guide Analysis

**Dataset:** 180 blog posts (June 2022 - December 2025)
**Total Sentences:** 6,988
**Total Words:** 168,201
**Analysis Date:** December 2025

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

David Knott's voice is characterized by:
- **Collaborative over authoritative** (1,121 "we" patterns vs 706 "I" patterns)
- **Heavily hedged** (8.67:1 hedging-to-assertion ratio)
- **Humble and self-deprecating** (frequent admissions of ignorance)
- **Transitional voice flow** (157 I→we transitions, 109 we→you transitions)
- **Question-driven openings** (12.2% of posts open with questions)
- **Topic-adaptive confidence** (highest hedging on quantum/architecture, lowest on cloud/people)

## 1. FIRST PERSON USAGE PATTERNS

### Overall Statistics
- **Total "I" occurrences:** 706 (3.9 per post)
- **10.1% of sentences** contain first-person constructions

### Breakdown by Type

#### A. OPINION MARKERS (Most Common)
| Pattern | Count | % of Sentences | Usage Context |
|---------|-------|----------------|---------------|
| **I have** | 226 | 3.23% | Experience citation, evidence |
| **I think** | 193 | 2.76% | Tentative opinion |
| **I believe** | 104 | 1.49% | Stronger conviction |
| **I can** | 59 | 0.84% | Capability/memory |

**Key Finding:** "I think that" is used **1.85x more frequently** than "I believe that"

#### B. Contextual Differences

**"I think that" (193 uses) - Exploratory/Tentative:**
- Used for working hypotheses
- Often precedes qualifiers ("perhaps", "may")
- Introduces ideas open to challenge
- Examples:
  - "I think that legacy transformation often makes sense, but we do not always do it for the best reasons"
  - "I think that these two examples help us think through the difference"

**"I believe that" (104 uses) - Conviction/Values:**
- Used for core principles
- Less likely to be hedged
- Introduces statements David stands behind
- Examples:
  - "I believe that this historical moment reinforces three important lessons"
  - "I believe that understanding the origins of cloud platforms helps us understand the value"

#### C. HUMILITY MARKERS

| Pattern | Count | Function |
|---------|-------|----------|
| **I must admit** | 11 | Reluctant concession |
| **I suspect** | 8 | Tentative hypothesis |
| **I find** | 14 | Personal observation |
| **I would** | 34 | Conditional opinion |

**Signature Examples:**
- "I must admit that I was sceptical"
- "I must admit that, if I hadn't made this commitment to learn in public, I might have given up"
- "I must admit are too big to fit in my head at the moment"

#### D. EXPERIENCE MARKERS

| Pattern | Count | Function |
|---------|-------|----------|
| **I have** | 226 | Evidence/authority |
| **I've** | 3 | Casual experience |
| **I remember** | 1 | Historical context |

**Usage Pattern:** "I have" serves dual purpose:
1. **Authority builder:** "I have seen that when we trust the team..."
2. **Evidence citation:** "I have learnt that analogies based on non-quantum phenomena can be dangerous"

### Voice Transition Analysis

**I → WE Transitions: 157 occurrences**

**Pattern:** David typically moves from personal observation/experience to collective challenge

**Example:**
> FROM: "I was also intrigued to learn that Voyager 1 is currently on its 'extended mission'"
> TO: "The idea of the extended mission immediately reminded me of the enterprise computer systems **we** build and run"

**Triggers for I→WE shift:**
1. Moving from anecdote to analysis
2. Generalizing from personal to universal
3. Invoking shared professional experience
4. Building collaborative framing

**WE → YOU Transitions: 109 occurrences**

**Pattern:** Moves from collective statement to direct address/actionable advice

**Example:**
> FROM: "But perhaps this is a self fulfilling prophecy: if **we** characterize legacy systems in this way..."
> TO: "If **your** motivation for modernisation is to make people want to work on your core systems, then **you** need to think..."

**Triggers for WE→YOU shift:**
1. Moving to specific recommendations
2. Conditional logic ("If you...")
3. Direct advice-giving
4. Personalizing abstract concepts

### Inclusive "We" Categories

**1. Professional Community "We" (most common)**
- "we build and run"
- "we have been working in enterprise computing"
- "As technologists, we..."

**2. Industry-Wide "We"**
- "we can show that typical funding models..."
- "we don't always do it for the best reasons"

**3. Universal "We"**
- "we all make mistakes"
- "we can't see software"

## 4. HEDGING LANGUAGE

### Overall Statistics
- **Total hedging occurrences:** 910 (5.1 per post)
- **13.0% of sentences** contain hedging language
- **8.67:1 hedging-to-assertion ratio**

### Hedging Vocabulary

| Hedge | Count | % of Sentences | Position/Usage |
|-------|-------|----------------|----------------|
| **may** | 275 | 3.94% | Most versatile hedge |
| **could** | 244 | 3.49% | Hypothetical possibility |
| **might** | 148 | 2.12% | Lower probability |
| **perhaps** | 93 | 1.33% | Thoughtful qualifier |
| **probably** | 46 | 0.66% | Likely but uncertain |
| **it seems** | 33 | 0.47% | Observational hedge |
| **maybe** | 29 | 0.41% | Casual uncertainty |
| **possibly** | 22 | 0.31% | Remote possibility |

### "Perhaps" Analysis (93 uses)

**Position in Sentence:**
- **Sentence-initial:** ~60% ("Perhaps we should...")
- **Mid-sentence:** ~40% ("...and perhaps we can...")

**Function:**
- Softens recommendations
- Invites reader consideration
- Signals thoughtful uncertainty

**Examples:**
- "Perhaps this means that, when considering our legacy modernisation strategies, we should look to the team before the systems"
- "Perhaps we go wrong, not by holding cloud to a higher standard..."

### Modal Verb Hedging: May/Might/Could

**"May" (275 uses) - Genuine Possibility:**
- "...then thoughtful refactoring, redesign or replacement of your legacy systems **may** make sense"
- "Those bad practices **may** be in the way we manage software"

**"Might" (148 uses) - Lower Certainty:**
- "We **might** suffer a data centre failure"
- "...which **might** never be needed"

**"Could" (244 uses) - Hypothetical/Conditional:**
- "if _only_ we **could** change the funding model"
- "They **could** no longer achieve everything they planned"

### Compound Hedging (Hedging × 2)

David frequently **stacks hedges** for maximum tentativeness:

- "**perhaps** we **can**"
- "**might** **possibly**"
- "I **think** it **seems** likely"
- "I **suspect** we **may**"

**Example:** "Perhaps our problem **isn't** that people are leaving, but that we **haven't** invested..."
(hedge + negative + hedge structure)

### Overall Statistics

### Assertion Vocabulary

| Assertion | Count | % of Sentences | Strength |
|-----------|-------|----------------|----------|
| **we must** | 49 | 0.70% | Moral imperative |
| **you should** | 17 | 0.24% | Directive |
| **certainly** | 12 | 0.17% | Confident affirmation |
| **clearly** | 11 | 0.16% | Obvious truth claim |
| **it is important** | 5 | 0.07% | Value statement |
| **obviously** | 5 | 0.07% | Self-evident claim |

### "We Must" - The Primary Assertion (49 uses)

**Usage Pattern:** Reserved for non-negotiable principles

**Examples:**
- "However, we must be clear about which skills are being lost"
- "Indeed, we must tell people that something has gone wrong"
- "If we think that we must preserve normality in every possible scenario..."

**Note:** Even strong assertions are often **contextualized or qualified**:
- "we must be clear" (epistemic duty, not action)
- "we must tell people" (ethical obligation, softened by "indeed")

### When David Makes Unhedged Assertions

**Rare instances of confident, unqualified statements occur when:**

1. **Stating obvious facts:** "Software is not an asset"
2. **Ethical imperatives:** "we must tell people"
3. **Definitional claims:** "Legacy isn't a technical problem, it's a management problem"
4. **Observed patterns:** "Practice takes practice"

**Pattern:** Strong assertions typically appear in:
- **Titles** (25% of titles are assertive)
- **Closing paragraphs** (building to conclusion)
- **Ethical/principle statements**

### Opening vs. Closing Confidence

| Position | Hedged | Assertive | Questions |
|----------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Opening** | 2.2% | 1.1% | 12.2% |
| **Closing** | ~15% | ~5% | ~8% |

**Key Finding:** Posts **open with questions or humility**, then **build toward modest assertions** at close.

**Function:** Signals awareness that "simple" solutions are anything but.

Often used **ironically** to signal the opposite:
- "Of course, this may be another dangerous 'just'"

## 7. READER RELATIONSHIP

### How David Positions the Reader

#### A. READER STANCE ANALYSIS

| Stance | Occurrences | % of Total |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Collaborative** | 557 | 65.7% |
| **Questioning** | 244 | 28.8% |
| **Authoritative** | 31 | 3.7% |
| **Peer** | 14 | 1.7% |
| **Empathetic** | 30 | 0.4% |

#### B. COLLABORATIVE STANCE (Most Dominant)

**Markers:**
- "let's"
- "together"
- "our"
- "we can"

**Examples:**
- "Unlike Voyager, though, **we** often lament the longevity of these systems. **We** regard them as antiquated..."
- "But perhaps this is a self fulfilling prophecy: if **we** characterize legacy systems in this way..."

**Function:** Positions David and reader as **co-travelers** in understanding technology challenges.

#### C. QUESTIONING STANCE (Second Most Common)

**Two Types of Questions:**

**1. Diagnostic Questions (reader self-assessment):**
- Title: "Do you know the difference between reliability and resilience?"
- "If you've been part of a development team, you will recognise..."
- "Are you breaking up monoliths or creating rubble?"

**2. Rhetorical Questions (prompting reflection):**
- "What if we behaved as if we cared about technology?"
- "How do we get people to care about computing?"
- "What if we could see software?"

**Pattern:** 12.2% of posts **open with questions**, establishing inquiry-driven tone.

#### D. AUTHORITATIVE STANCE (Rare - 3.7%)

**Reserved for specific contexts:**
- Non-negotiable principles
- Safety/ethics issues
- Correcting misconceptions

**Examples:**
- "Together, these differences between reliability and resilience teach us one final, important lesson"
- "you should identify the key leadership roles"
- "Above the line, focus on building capabilities that you should be great at"

**Note:** Even "authoritative" statements are **hedged or contextualized**.

#### E. PEER STANCE (Explicitly Rare - 1.7%)

**"As technologists" / "We all":**
- "We all make mistakes, especially when we are building and running enterprise technology"
- "A diagram this simple also needs a few words of justification. We all know that..."

**Function:** Establishes shared professional identity, but used sparingly.

### Assumed Knowledge Level

**David writes for readers who:**

1. **Have technical context but not expertise**
   - Explains quantum computing from scratch
   - Defines specialized terms
   - Provides analogies for complex concepts

2. **Work in enterprise technology**
   - Assumes familiarity with: DevOps, cloud, architecture, legacy systems
   - Does not explain: basic programming, agile, common patterns

3. **Care about "why" not just "how"**
   - Focuses on principles over procedures
   - Emphasizes understanding over recipes

4. **Appreciate learning journeys**
   - Comfortable with "I don't know yet"
   - Value process over perfection

### Reader as Co-Learner

**Key characteristic:** David positions readers as **fellow travelers**, not students.

**Evidence:**
- Shares his own learning struggles
- Asks questions without always providing answers
- Uses "we" when exploring difficult topics
- Admits when concepts are "too big to fit in my head"

**Effect:** Reader feels **invited into discovery** rather than lectured.

## 10. ACTIONABLE VOICE RULES

### RULE 1: HEDGE BY DEFAULT, ASSERT SPARINGLY

**Target Ratio:** 8:1 hedging to assertion

**Implementation:**
- Use "may," "might," "could" liberally
- Reserve "must," "should," "clearly" for non-negotiables
- Default to "I think" over "I believe"
- Prefer "perhaps we" over "we should"

**When to Assert:**
- Ethical imperatives
- Observed facts from experience
- Definitional statements
- Closing summaries (after building case)

---

### RULE 2: TRANSITION I → WE → YOU

**Standard Flow:**
1. **Open with "I"** (personal observation, anecdote, experience)
2. **Shift to "we"** (generalize to shared professional context)
3. **Close with "you"** (specific, actionable guidance)

**Example Structure:**
> "**I** was intrigued to learn..." [personal]
> "The idea reminded me of the systems **we** build..." [collective]
> "If **your** motivation is X, then **you** need to..." [actionable]

**Transition Triggers:**
- I→WE: Moving from anecdote to analysis
- WE→YOU: Moving from principle to practice

---

### RULE 3: QUALIFY OPINIONS EXPLICITLY

**Hierarchy of Opinion Markers:**

| Strength | Phrase | Usage |
|----------|--------|-------|
| **Tentative** | "I think," "perhaps," "it seems" | Default position |
| **Moderate** | "I believe," "I suspect," "likely" | After consideration |
| **Strong** | "I would argue," "clearly," "must" | Rare, defended |

**Implementation:**
- Never make naked assertions without qualification
- Stack qualifiers when genuinely uncertain: "I think it might perhaps..."
- Use "I must admit" when conceding difficulty

---

### RULE 4: ADMIT IGNORANCE FREELY

**Acceptable Self-Deprecation:**
- "I have no idea"
- "I must admit"
- "I struggle to understand"
- "too big to fit in my head"
- "the breadth of my ignorance expands"

**Function:** Builds trust, models learning, invites reader into discovery

**Frequency:** ~1 per post minimum

---

### RULE 5: OPEN WITH QUESTIONS

**Target:** 12% of posts should open with questions

**Question Types:**
1. **Diagnostic:** "Do you know the difference between...?"
2. **Rhetorical:** "What if we behaved as if...?"
3. **Exploratory:** "How do we get people to care...?"

**Benefit:** Engages reader, sets collaborative tone, frames inquiry

---

### RULE 6: USE "IF YOU" FOR CONDITIONAL GUIDANCE

**Target:** 3.6% of sentences

**Structure:**
- "If you [situation], then you [action/consequence]"
- "If you [have X goal], think about [Y consideration]"

**Examples:**
- "If you've been part of a development team, you will recognise..."
- "If your motivation is X, then Y makes sense"

**Benefit:** Personalizes advice, respects reader context, avoids blanket prescriptions

---

### RULE 7: CALIBRATE CONFIDENCE BY TOPIC

**Personal Expertise (Cloud/People):**
- Hedge ratio: 1.5:1
- More "should," "must," "important"
- Still maintain qualifiers

**Learning Domains (Quantum/AI):**
- Hedge ratio: 2.0:1
- More "perhaps," "seems," "might"
- Explicit about novice status

**Complex Theory (Architecture):**
- Hedge ratio: 1.9:1
- Heavy use of "could," "may"
- Acknowledge multiple perspectives

---

### RULE 8: DEPLOY WIT THROUGH STRUCTURAL IRONY

**Techniques:**

**A. The "Just" Irony:**
- Flag naive simplifications: "we 'just' need to..."
- Signal awareness of complexity

**B. "Of Course" Reversals:**
- "Of course, this may be another dangerous 'just'"
- Signals the opposite of obviousness

**C. "Unfortunately" Observations:**
- Wry acknowledgment of systemic problems
- Gentle, rueful tone

---

### RULE 9: POSITION READER AS PEER, NOT STUDENT

**Collaborative Markers:**
- "we" (65.7% of relational language)
- "let's"
- "together"
- "our shared challenge"

**Avoid:**
- Lecturing tone
- "You must learn"
- Talking down
- Assumed ignorance

**Instead:**
- "We all struggle with this"
- "I'm still learning"
- "Perhaps we can explore"

---

**Opening Pattern (first paragraph):**
- 23.3% use "I" (personal)
- 12.2% use questions
- 2.2% hedged
- 1.1% assertive

**Closing Pattern (final paragraph):**
- ~15% hedged

## 11. ANTI-PATTERNS (What David DOESN'T Do)

### ❌ NEVER:
1. **Open with strong assertions** (1.1% of opens)
2. **Use "obviously" or "clearly" without qualification** (16 total uses across 180 posts)
3. **Give unqualified directives** ("you must" = 2 uses only)
